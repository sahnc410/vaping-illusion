Illusion 04 · The real numbers

The headline: current e-cigarette use among high schoolers collapsed from 27.5% in 2019 to 7.8% in 2024 — real progress, driven partly by policy (flavor restrictions, Tobacco 21) and changing norms. But progress at the population level hides what's happening to the teens still vaping. Click view ② .

Source: National Youth Tobacco Survey (CDC/FDA), 2019–2024. "Current use" = past 30 days.

The twist: fewer teens vape, but those who do are far more addicted. Daily use among youth vapers nearly doubled (15% → 29%), and among daily users, the share who tried to quit but couldn't rose from 28% to 53%. Prevention now has two jobs: keep teens from starting, and take "I can stop whenever" off the table.

Source: Monitoring the Future analysis published in JAMA Network Open, U.S. secondary students, 2020–2024.

On paper, Asian Americans smoke the least of any group. One number, one story. Press the button to see what that average is hiding.

Sources: aggregate rate from national adult surveys (CDC); Korean American subgroup rates from community and state studies (e.g., California Health Interview Survey era research) — reported ranges, shown here to illustrate masking. Exact figures vary by study, year, and region; that variation is itself part of the problem.

Flavors are the on-ramp, disposables are the vehicle. Nearly 9 in 10 teen vapers use flavored products — fruit, candy, mint — and a majority reach for cheap disposables built for continuous use. This is why flavor policy (view ⑤ of the timeline below) sits at the center of youth prevention.

Source: National Youth Tobacco Survey (CDC/FDA), 2024 — flavored product use among current youth e-cigarette users.

Prevention is a moving target. As vaping falls, nicotine pouches (like Zyn) are climbing among older teens — use among 12th graders roughly tripled in two years. Any campaign built only around "vaping" will age fast; ours is built around nicotine and the illusions that sell it, whatever the package.

Source: Monitoring the Future, University of Michigan — past-12-month nicotine pouch use, 12th grade, 2023–2025.

Illusion 05 · "Nothing can be done"

Policy works. Here's the NYC record.

The fall in teen vaping didn't just happen — New York built some of the strongest youth tobacco policy in the country, and the data above moved with it. A short timeline, and where it goes next.

2014

NYC raises the tobacco age to 21

New York City becomes one of the first major U.S. cities with "Tobacco 21," years ahead of the federal law — cutting off the social supply chain where 16-year-olds got products from 18-year-old classmates.

2018

Retail density caps & price floors

NYC caps tobacco retail licenses to shrink the number of sellers over time, sets minimum prices, and requires e-cigarette retailers to hold licenses — making nicotine harder to stumble into on every corner.

2019

Pharmacies stop selling tobacco · Federal Tobacco 21

Tobacco disappears from NYC drugstores, and the federal minimum age rises to 21 nationwide — following the path NYC helped open.

2020

New York bans flavored vapes

New York State prohibits the sale of flavored e-cigarettes — targeting the single biggest on-ramp for youth use (see dashboard view ④). NYC pairs it with restrictions on other flavored tobacco products.

Now

The unfinished part

Enforcement against illegal flavored disposables (many imported and unauthorized), the rise of nicotine pouches, and online sales are the live battlegrounds. This is where our project's policy recommendations will land — informed by our own survey data on how teens actually encounter these products.

About this project

One thesis, six tracks.

Everything on this site is one argument — vaping is sold on illusions, and making them visible is prevention — built to cover every track of the KCS × NYU Langone program.

Student-led website

This site: a hand-built interactive platform, no templates, hosting all project work.

Research & academic

The perception-gap survey above generates our original data; the dashboard synthesizes NYTS, MTF, and community studies for our evidence-based paper.

Digital media campaign

Every section is a shareable asset: "1 in 17," the cost calculator, and myth cards become the peer-to-peer social campaign.

Creative communication

Complex health data translated into interactive visuals — dot grids, growing charts, flip cards — instead of lectures.

Expert engagement

Survey design and the disaggregation analysis are reviewed with NYU Langone population-health mentors before deployment.

Policy & advocacy (NYC)

The policy timeline tracks NYC/NYS law; our recommendations will target enforcement gaps our survey uncovers.
